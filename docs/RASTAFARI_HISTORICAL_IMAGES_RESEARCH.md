## Leonard Howell - The First Rasta

### Who He Was
**Leonard Percival Howell** (1898-1981) is widely recognized as the **first Rasta** and founder of the Rastafari movement. Born in Jamaica, he traveled extensively (including to the United States and Africa) before returning to Jamaica in 1932 to preach the divinity of Haile Selassie I.

### Historical Significance
- **1933:** Arrested for sedition for preaching that Haile Selassie was the living God and Black people should pledge allegiance to Ethiopia, not the British Crown
- **1940:** Founded the **Pinnacle commune**, the first organized Rastafari community
- **Pioneer:** Established the foundational theology, symbols, and practices of Rastafari
- **Ganja Sacrament:** Institutionalized cannabis use as a spiritual practice at Pinnacle
- **Economic Independence:** Created a self-sustaining agricultural community that challenged colonial authority

- **Visual Context:** Shows the dignity and presence of the man who defied the British Empire

### Why This Matters for Ganja Mon
Leonard Howell represents the **original rebel spirit** - someone who built an autonomous system (Pinnacle) that challenged the establishment. Ganja Mon's autonomous cultivation system echoes this independence. Howell's use of ganja as sacrament directly connects to Mon's cannabis cultivation mission.

## The Pinnacle Commune

### What It Was
**Pinnacle** was a 500-acre hilltop commune in Sligoville, St. Catherine Parish, Jamaica, founded by Leonard Howell in 1940. It was the **first organized Rastafari community** and housed up to 1,600 residents at its peak.

### Historical Significance
- **Autonomous Society:** Self-governing community with its own economy, laws, and spiritual practices
- **Ganja Cultivation:** Large-scale cannabis farming for spiritual and economic purposes
- **Persecution:** Raided repeatedly by Jamaican police (1941, 1954, 1958) due to ganja cultivation and anti-colonial ideology
- **1954 Destruction:** Final major raid destroyed the commune, scattering the Rastafari community into Kingston ghettos
- **Cultural Incubator:** Many early Rastafari elders and musicians (including Count Ossie) emerged from Pinnacle

## Marcus Garvey - The Prophet

### Who He Was
**Marcus Mosiah Garvey** (1887-1940) was a Jamaican political activist, publisher, and founder of the **Universal Negro Improvement Association (UNIA)** and the **Black Star Line** shipping company. Though not a Rastafari himself, he is revered as a prophet of the movement.

### Historical Significance
- **Pan-Africanism:** Advocated for Black economic independence and repatriation to Africa
- **Prophecy:** His statement "Look to Africa, where a black king shall be crowned" was interpreted as predicting Haile Selassie's 1930 coronation
- **UNIA:** Built the largest Black mass movement in history (millions of members worldwide)
- **Black Star Line:** Attempted to create Black-owned international shipping (controversial due to financial collapse)
- **Persecution:** Imprisoned in the U.S. on mail fraud charges (1923), deported to Jamaica (1927)

## Prince Emmanuel Charles Edwards - Bobo Ashanti Founder

### Who He Was
**Prince Emmanuel Charles Edwards** (1915-1994) founded the **Bobo Ashanti** (Bobo Shanti) mansion, one of the three main Rastafari orders, in 1958. He established the community in **Bull Bay**, east of Kingston.

### Historical Significance
- **Strictest Order:** Bobo Ashanti is the most orthodox Rastafari mansion, with strict dietary laws, Sabbath observance, and separation from Babylon
- **Turbans and Robes:** Distinctive dress code (turbans, long robes) symbolizing priestly status
- **Trinity Theology:** Teaches that Haile Selassie (God), Marcus Garvey (Prophet), and Prince Emmanuel (High Priest) form a holy trinity
- **Economic Self-Sufficiency:** Bobo community produces brooms, crafts, and other goods for sale
- **Ganja Sacrament:** Maintains strict ritual use of cannabis

## Claudius Henry - The 1960 Treason Trial

### Who He Was
**Claudius Henry** was a Rastafari preacher who led a controversial repatriation movement in 1959-1960. His actions led to one of the most significant legal persecutions of Rastafari in Jamaican history.

### Historical Significance
- **1959 Repatriation Promise:** Sold thousands of "repatriation cards" promising followers they would sail to Africa on October 5, 1959
- **Failed Prophecy:** When the ships never arrived, followers were left destitute and disillusioned
- **1960 Raid:** Police raided his compound and found weapons, ammunition, and letters allegedly planning insurrection
- **Treason Trial:** Henry and his son Ronald were charged with treason (capital offense)
- **Persecution Catalyst:** The trial intensified government crackdown on all Rastafari, leading to widespread harassment

## Ronald Henry - First Afrika Corps

### Who He Was
**Ronald Henry** (Claudius Henry's son) was a Rastafari militant who formed the **First Afrika Corps**, a guerrilla group that operated in the Red Hills area of Jamaica in 1960.

### Historical Significance
- **Armed Resistance:** After his father's arrest, Ronald formed a guerrilla unit to resist the Jamaican government
- **Red Hills Guerrilla Campaign:** The group engaged in armed confrontations with police in the hills above Kingston
- **Casualties:** Several British soldiers were killed in ambushes, escalating the crisis
- **Capture and Execution:** Ronald Henry was captured, tried, and **hanged** in 1960 for murder
- **Rastafari Persecution:** The incident led to the **Coral Gardens Massacre** and years of brutal suppression

## Coral Gardens Massacre - 1963 Persecution

### What Happened
The **Coral Gardens Incident** (April 11-12, 1963) was a violent confrontation between Rastafari and Jamaican security forces in Montego Bay, followed by a brutal government crackdown. It is considered the **worst persecution** in Rastafari history.

### Historical Significance
- **Initial Incident:** A dispute at a gas station escalated into violence; several people (including police) were killed
- **Government Response:** Declared a state of emergency; mass arrests of Rastafari across Jamaica
- **Brutality:** Rastafari were beaten, forcibly shaved (cutting dreadlocks was a profound violation), and imprisoned without trial
- **Hundreds Detained:** Estimates range from 150-1,000 Rastafari arrested
- **Long-Term Trauma:** The massacre created lasting fear and mistrust of authorities
- **2017 Apology:** The Jamaican government officially apologized 54 years later
